Quick Overview: What is CareBridge Companion?

CareBridge Companion is an on-site AI-powered youth support system designed to help youth in group living facilities communicate openly about their feelings, experiences, and concerns. The system generates information that helps care teams respond faster, more consistently, and more compassionately to youth needs.

In simple terms:

  • Youth talk to a supportive AI companion (like a caring listener available 24/7)
  • The system watches for safety concerns and alerts staff when something matters
  • Staff get summaries of patterns instead of having to read everything
  • The whole facility can respond more consistently across shifts and teams

Why CareBridge Companion Matters to Your Work

The Problem It Solves

In group living, several things happen:

  • Context gets lost between shifts when different staff are on duty
  • Youth may open up outside therapy hours with concerns that fall through cracks
  • Handoffs are inconsistent — what matters to one shift may get missed by the next
  • Staff document differently — making it hard to spot patterns
  • Youth may not feel safe talking to some staff members about certain things

How CareBridge Helps

  • 24/7 availability — Youth can check-in anytime, no appointment needed
  • Consistent briefings — Care team gets the same story, not fragmented information
  • Faster pattern detection — System flags escalating concerns (e.g., increasing hopelessness, changing mood)
  • Better preparation — Therapists and supervisors know what to focus on
  • Reduced documentation burden — Structured summaries instead of reading logs
  • Equal voice — All youth get the same access, reducing feeling invisible or unheard
  • LGBTQ+ affirming — Youth of all identities see themselves represented in language and assumptions

What CareBridge Companion IS and ISN'T

✅ What It IS
  • A supportive listening tool where youth feel heard
  • A system that helps identify patterns and timing of concerns
  • A briefing tool to prepare staff for conversations with youth
  • A safety alert system for high-risk messages
  • Part of your facility's care infrastructure
  • A tool to reduce the burden on direct-care staff
❌ What It ISN'T
  • Not a therapist — Provides supportive conversation, not clinical treatment
  • Not a crisis hotline — Safety alerts need staff follow-up
  • Not a staff messaging system — Staff cannot send messages to youth
  • Not private from facility oversight — All conversations are facility records
  • Not a replacement for human connection — It's a tool that supports your work
  • Not automated action — Alerts still require human judgment

Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) — Our Core Commitment

CareBridge Companion is designed from the ground up with a commitment to Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion. This isn't an add-on; it's foundational to how the system works and how you use it.

Why DEI Matters in Care

In group living, youth from marginalized backgrounds often face:

  • Invisibility — Their experiences and identities overlooked
  • Bias — Stereotypes that affect care decisions
  • Barriers — Discrimination that prevents open communication
  • Chilling effects — Fear of consequences for disclosing identity or experiences

CareBridge is designed to counteract these patterns.

DEI Principles Built Into the System

1. Diversity — All Youth Belong Here

  • The companion uses affirming language from the start, regardless of identity
  • Youth can share about any aspect of themselves — sexuality, gender, race, religion, family structure, disability — without judgment
  • Briefings preserve context about youth identity and background (when relevant to care)
  • System design recognizes youth hold multiple identities at once (e.g., Black LGBTQ+ youth, disabled immigrant youth)

2. Equity — Everyone Gets What They Need

  • All youth have equal access — no barriers based on cost, technology, language
  • 24/7 availability means youth who are isolated or unsupervised at night can reach out
  • Customized briefings adapted to individual youth's needs and identity
  • Fair safety monitoring that doesn't over-flag marginalized youth for normal experiences (crying, discussing discrimination, exploring identity)

3. Inclusion — Youth Feel Safe and Affirmed

  • Staff cannot message youth (no grooming, no inappropriate contact)
  • All staff access is logged and monitored to prevent targeting
  • Quarterly bias reviews ensure alerts are fair across all youth
  • Youth privacy around identity is protected — information only shared when necessary for care

What This Means for Your Work

Affirming Language & Attitude

  • Use chosen names and pronouns — always
  • Don't make assumptions about family, relationships, or identity
  • Recognize that exploring identity is normal, healthy development, not a problem
  • Validate diverse backgrounds, cultures, religions, and family structures

Recognizing Bias (Including Your Own)

  • Be aware that you may have unconscious biases — we all do
  • Don't let assumptions about appearance, background, or identity drive your care
  • Ask yourself: "Am I responding differently to this youth because of their identity?"
  • Listen to youth when they tell you they've experienced discrimination

Equitable Care Across the Team

  • Use the briefing summaries to give all youth consistent attention
  • Don't let one youth dominate your focus while others get overlooked
  • Make sure quieter or less "visible" youth still get checked on
  • Notice if certain youth are flagged more often — bring it up with your supervisor

Equity in Escalation

  • When you escalate a concern, check your own bias
  • Would you escalate the same message from a different youth?
  • Are you treating LGBTQ+ youth disclosure as "crisis" when it's just self-disclosure?
  • Are you over-responding to behaviors that are normal for some youth?

LGBTQ+ Affirming Care (Specific Commitment)

For LGBTQ+ youth specifically:

✅ What to Do:
  • Honor chosen names and pronouns, 100% of the time
  • Create space for youth to explore identity safely
  • Validate LGBTQ+ identity and relationships as normal
  • Protect identity information (especially in unsupportive environments)
  • Escalate discrimination or identity-based harassment immediately
❌ What NOT to Do:
  • Pathologize LGBTQ+ identity ("Is this phase?" or "Are you sure?")
  • Correct pronouns or use deadnames
  • Allow other youth to bully or harass LGBTQ+ peers
  • Share identity information without consent
  • Assume religious belief overrides affirming practice

More detail: See LGBTQ_AFFIRMING_POLICY.md for comprehensive commitments.

Quarterly Bias Monitoring (Everyone's Job)

Your facility should review quarterly:

  • Are safety alerts distributed fairly? Are certain groups flagged more?
  • Do briefings respect identity? Or do they reinforce stereotypes?
  • Is break-glass access used appropriately? Any suspicious patterns?
  • Have any youth reported discrimination? By staff or peers?

You can contribute by:

  • Providing feedback if an alert seems off
  • Flagging bias you notice in briefings or alerts
  • Letting leadership know if a youth reports discrimination
  • Being honest in staff conversations about your own biases

How the System Works: A Day in the Life

Morning Briefing

6:00 AM — Overnight supervisor comes on shift and checks the CareBridge dashboard

  • 3 new messages flagged overnight (1 medium alert, 2 routine)
  • Briefing summary: "Kai reported feeling lonely; Marcus sleeping poorly (3rd night); Jordan mentioned family call stress"
  • Supervisor quickly reviews actual messages for Marcus, plans check-in before breakfast

7:30 AM — Direct-care staff gets casual briefing during shift meeting

  • "Just so you know, Marcus isn't sleeping great; extra attention today"
  • You know context before talking to him, can ask better questions, respond more thoughtfully

During the Day

10:00 AM — Youth starts a conversation with the companion

  • Talks about peer conflict, feeling frustrated, worried about group outing
  • Companion listens, asks clarifying questions, suggests coping strategies
  • System detects medium concern (acute frustration but no safety red flags)
  • No immediate alert; message goes into daily briefing

2:00 PM — A medium alert arrives at staff

  • Different youth disclosed possible abuse indicator in their chat
  • Alert goes to supervisor and therapist immediately: "Potential abuse indicator — see message"
  • You don't see the full transcript (privacy control) but know to check on them
  • You follow facility protocol: supervision, careful conversation, reporting to safeguarding

Evening Shift

5:00 PM — You start your evening shift, check the system

  • Briefing tells you what happened during day: "Kai and Marcus are doing okay; check on Jordan before family call"
  • You're prepared for the shift without detailed reading

8:00 PM — You respond to a safety alert

  • A youth expressed feeling hopeless and mentioned "not wanting to be here"
  • You follow your facility's protocol: immediate supervision, safety assessment, notification
  • System logged the alert time, message content, and your response

Staff Consistency

Because all staff see the same information in the same way:

  • Night shift knows what day shift knows
  • Relief staff know the context
  • New staff member gets up to speed faster
  • Youth experience consistency: "Everyone seems to know what's going on with me"

Key Features You'll Use

1. Daily Briefings

What: A short summary of the last 24 hours (or filtered by time range)

DAILY BRIEFING — March 3, 2026 (6:00 AM) MOOD TRENDS: - Kai: anxiety increasing (unusual for her) - Jordan: withdrawn, less engagement + Marcus: mood improved compared to yesterday THEMES: - Family contact (4 youth mentioned) - Peer conflict (2 incidents) - Sleep issues (3 youth) SUGGESTED ACTIONS: - Check in with Kai before group time - Jordan might be ready for one-on-one - Encourage Marcus (improvement is good) TIMING: Overnight alerts routed to on-call clinician

What you do with it:

  • Prepare for the day
  • Know who needs extra attention
  • Approach conversations with context
  • Coordinate with your team

2. Safety Alerts

What: Immediate notification when system detects high-risk language or disclosures

Examples that trigger alerts:

  • Self-harm language ("I want to hurt myself")
  • Abuse or exploitation indicators ("My uncle..."; "He told me not to tell")
  • Crisis language ("I can't do this anymore"; "I don't want to be here")
  • Urgent medical concerns ("Can't breathe"; "I took pills")
  • Concerning patterns ("This is the 5th message about wanting to run")

What you do:

  1. Read the alert (includes severity: medium/high/critical)
  2. Follow your facility's protocol (supervision, assessment, notification)
  3. Take action or escalate
  4. Document what you did
  5. System logs everything for audit trail

3. Conversation Details

What: Ability to view youth's messages and system analysis

You can see:

  • Full conversation (messages youth sent to the companion)
  • Mood analysis ("Reported anxiety, frustration; emotional intensity high")
  • Themes detected ("Family concerns, peer relationships")
  • Safety flags applied ("Potential self-harm indicators")
  • Suggested actions from the system
Privacy note: Some details are minimized unless you need them for safety/care. If you need more information, you can request break-glass access with documented reason and your action is logged.

4. Notes & Documentation

What: Ability to add staff observations to a youth's record

Examples:

  • "Checked in after alert; youth seemed less anxious after walk"
  • "Family call happened 3 PM; seemed to help mood"
  • "Mentioned homework stress; offered tutoring"
  • "Sleep pattern improving after routine change"

Why it matters: Your observation + system data = fuller picture for clinicians and next shift

5. Dashboard & Statistics

What: Overview of system activity and trends (if facility shows you)

Might include:

  • Total check-ins this week
  • Youth engagement rate
  • Alert frequency and types
  • Most common themes (family, peer, school, identity)
  • Trend arrows (improving/declining)

Why it matters: Helps leadership see if the system is helping, where to focus resources

Understanding Youth Privacy & Boundaries

What Youth Know

  • Their conversations are with the companion
  • Staff can see summaries and alerts
  • The facility is monitoring for safety (age-appropriate explanation)
  • Some messages might trigger alerts to staff (so staff can help)
  • Their identity is protected in how information is shared

What Staff Can & Can't See

✅ You CAN See ❌ You CAN'T See (unless authorized)
Briefings (summaries of themes and mood) Full transcript of all messages
Safety alerts (with message excerpts when critical) Identity-sensitive disclosures
Overall patterns and trends Certain details (like specific names mentioned)
Notes you or other staff have added Conversations without safety reason

Break-Glass Access (Safeguarding/Investigation)

  • Only admin, safeguarding, or authorized leadership
  • Requires documented reason ("We think this youth may be experiencing abuse")
  • Your action is logged with timestamp and reason
  • Used for investigations or critical safety decisions

Minimum Necessary Principle

  • On-call clinician during crisis gets more detail than general staff
  • Staff on the unit see briefings; therapist sees more for session prep
  • Everyone's access is logged and monitored
  • You only see what you need to do your job safely and well

Common Staff Scenarios

Scenario 1: Morning Briefing Alert

Situation: You see a briefing note: "Sam reported feeling very sad and hopeless"

✅ What you should do:
  1. Plan a check-in before group activities
  2. Ask gentle, open questions ("How are you doing today?")
  3. Listen without judgment
  4. If concerning language present, follow safety protocol
  5. Document your observation
❌ What NOT to do:
  • Assume the system diagnosis is correct
  • Treat it like a crisis if system says "medium alert"
  • Ask about specific messages ("I saw you told the companion...")
  • Share details with other youth
Scenario 2: Critical Safety Alert

Situation: You receive alert: "CRITICAL — Self-harm language detected in message from Alex"

✅ What you should do:
  1. Read the alert message immediately
  2. Follow your facility's safety protocol (immediate steps depend on facility)
  3. If not on duty, notify supervisor/on-call clinician immediately
  4. Ensure direct supervision if indicated
  5. Document time, what you saw, what you did
  6. Escalate per mandatory reporting if needed

Time frame: Critical alerts = respond within minutes

Scenario 3: Youth Asks About the System

Situation: Jordan asks, "Why does everyone know about my conversations?"

✅ What to explain:
  • "We use a system that helps us understand what youth need and respond faster"
  • "The companion listens and summarizes what matters; we use that to help better"
  • "Safety is the main reason we monitor — if you mention hurting yourself, we need to know"
  • "You can talk about anything; we're here to help, not judge"
  • "Your identity and private details are protected — we don't share everything with everyone"
❌ What NOT to say:
  • "Big Brother is watching"
  • "Everything you say goes to staff"
  • "It's just a robot, not real help"
  • "You have no privacy here"
Scenario 4: You Disagree With a System Flag

Situation: System flags Casey's message "I'm so tired of this" as concerning, but you know Casey is just tired from a late homework assignment.

✅ What to do:
  1. Use your professional judgment (you know Casey better than the system)
  2. Check on Casey anyway ("You seem tired — everything okay?")
  3. Note in system that context was assignment, not mental health concern
  4. Provide feedback helps the system improve
  5. Trust yourself; the system is a tool, not a replacement for your expertise

Key Principles to Remember

1. System ≠ Judgment

The system flags patterns and language, not fixed assessments. A "high alert" doesn't mean the youth is in crisis; it means careful attention is needed. You make the final call.

2. Human Care Comes First

The system is a tool to support your expertise and judgment, not replace it. You know your youth. Trust yourself.

3. Context Matters

The system doesn't know the full story (family dynamics, historical patterns you've observed, their sense of humor). Your judgment + system data = better decisions.

4. Privacy with Purpose

Youth information is protected and accessed minimally. Breaches of privacy undermine trust in the whole system. Guard youth information like you'd want yours guarded.

5. Feedback Improves Everything

When you see an error or have an idea, speak up. The system learns from your corrections. Your insight makes the system better.
