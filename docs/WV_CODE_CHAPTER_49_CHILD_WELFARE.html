Overview

West Virginia Code Chapter 49 provides comprehensive legislation governing child welfare services, child protection, juvenile justice, and the rights of parents and families in the state. This code establishes:

  • Purpose and structure of the state's child welfare system
  • Definitions of child abuse, neglect, and related conditions
  • Procedures for child protection and family services
  • Parental rights and responsibilities
  • Licensing and approval of child care facilities
  • Systemic reporting and transparency requirements
  • Protections for parents and guardians

The code emphasizes family-centered services, child safety, and community-based interventions in the least restrictive settings possible.

Article 1: General Provisions and Definitions

§49-1-101: Short Title and Intent

File Code: §49-1-101

This chapter is known and cited as the "West Virginia Child Welfare Act."

The recodification during the 2015 legislative session is intended to consolidate and codify all state child welfare laws in revised form without altering substantive law or increasing funding obligations.

§49-1-102: Legislative Intent

File Code: §49-1-102

In the 2015 recodification:

  • Each reenactment continues the intended meaning of prior provisions
  • Existing judicial interpretations are preserved
  • No substantive changes to child welfare law are intended
  • No increase in funding obligations to state or local agencies

§49-1-105: Purpose of the Child Welfare System

File Code: §49-1-105

The state has a duty to assure that proper and appropriate care is given and maintained for children.

The child welfare and juvenile justice system shall:

  1. Assure each child care, safety and guidance
  2. Serve the mental and physical welfare of the child
  3. Preserve and strengthen child-family ties
  4. Recognize fundamental rights of children and parents
  5. Develop family-focused (not individual-focused) procedures and programs
  6. Involve the child, family, or caregiver in planning and service delivery
  7. Provide community-based services in least restrictive settings
  8. Provide early identification of problems and prevent abuse/neglect
  9. Provide rehabilitation for status offenders and juvenile delinquents
  10. Provide secure detention when necessary for alleged/adjudicated delinquents
  11. Protect public welfare

§49-1-106: Location of Services

File Code: §49-1-106

Child welfare services are located within and administered by the Bureau for Social Services within the Department of Human Services.

The Department of Human Services is designated as the state entity to:

  • Cooperate with federal health and justice agencies
  • Comply with federal regulations
  • Receive and expend federal funds

Part II: Key Definitions

§49-1-201: Definitions Related to Child Abuse and Neglect

File Code: §49-1-201
"Abused Child" means: A child whose health or welfare is being harmed or threatened by:
  • Physical injury or mental/emotional injury inflicted by a parent, guardian, or custodian (including excessive corporal punishment)
  • Sexual abuse or sexual exploitation
  • Sale or attempted sale in violation of WV Code
  • Domestic violence (as defined in WV Code §48-27-202)
  • Human trafficking or attempted trafficking
"Neglected Child" means: A child whose physical or mental health is harmed or threatened by:
  • Present refusal, failure, or inability to provide necessary food, clothing, shelter, supervision, medical care, or education
  • Absence or disappearance of parent/custodian leaving child without necessities

Note: Does not apply to children whose education is conducted under home education provisions (§18-8-1 et seq.)

"Sexual Abuse" means:
  • Sexual intercourse, intrusion, or contact (proscribed by WV Code §61-8c-3) engaged in or procured by a parent/guardian/custodian with a child
  • Displaying sex organs to a child for sexual gratification or to affront/alarm the child
  • Certain criminal offenses (WV Code §61-8b-7, -8, -9)
"Sexual Exploitation" means:
  • Persuading, inducing, enticing, or coercing a child to engage in sexually explicit conduct
  • Persuading a child to display sex organs for gratification or under circumstances likely to be observed by others
  • Knowingly maintaining or making available a child for commercial sexual activity
"Serious Physical Abuse" means: Bodily injury that creates substantial risk of death, causes serious/prolonged disfigurement, prolonged impairment of health, or prolonged loss/impairment of bodily function.
"Imminent Danger to Physical Well-Being" means: An emergency situation where the welfare or life of the child is threatened, including:
  • Nonaccidental trauma inflicted by caretaker
  • Pattern of abuse (battered child syndrome)
  • Nutritional deprivation or abandonment
  • Inadequate treatment of serious illness/disease
  • Substantial emotional injury inflicted by parent/guardian/custodian
  • Sale or attempted sale of the child
  • Parent/guardian alcohol/drug abuse impairing parenting skills to pose imminent risk
  • Any other condition threatening health, life, or safety

§49-1-202: Definitions Related to Child, Adult, and Status

File Code: §49-1-202
"Child" or "Juvenile" means: Any person under 18 years of age or a transitioning adult. For child care services:
  • Under 13 years of age, OR
  • 13-18 years of age and under court supervision, OR
  • 13-18 years of age with significant developmental delay
"Transitioning Adult" means: An individual 18-21 years of age who:
  • Was in custody of Department of Human Services at age 18, committed delinquency before 18, and remains under juvenile court jurisdiction; OR
  • Was adjudicated abused/neglected or in DHS custody at age 18 and enters contract to continue in educational/training program initiated before age 18
"Juvenile Delinquent" means: A juvenile adjudicated as one who commits an act that would be a crime if committed by an adult.
"Status Offender" means: A juvenile adjudicated as one who:
  • Habitually refuses lawful supervision endangering health/safety, OR
  • Left care without consent/good cause, OR
  • Is habitually absent from school without good cause

Part III: Licensing and Approval

§49-1-203: Licensing and Approval Definitions

File Code: §49-1-203

The code defines several important regulatory designations:

"License" means official permission to engage in an activity that would otherwise be prohibited.
"Approval" means a finding that a state-operated facility has met requirements and a certificate of approval/operation has been issued.
"Registration" means official permission to a family child care home, informal family child care home, or relative family child care home determined to be in compliance with legislative rules.

The code establishes that regulation seeks to balance:

  • Child protection — Protecting users from risks they cannot self-protect against
  • Provider interests — Not over-encumbering quality providers with excessive licensure requirements
  • Proportionality — Regulations proportionate to facility size

Part IV: Custodians, Guardians, and Family

§49-1-204: Family-Related Definitions

File Code: §49-1-204
"Caregiver" means any person at least 18 years of age who is:
  • Related by blood, marriage, or adoption (but not legal custodian/guardian), OR
  • Has resided with the minor continuously for 6+ months
"Custodian" means a person who has or shares actual physical possession or care/custody of a child, regardless of legal custody status.

These definitions recognize the various family structures and caregiving arrangements present in modern families.

Article 11: Systemic Reporting Transparency

§49-11-101: Child Welfare Data Dashboard

File Code: §49-11-101

Effective July 1, 2026: The Commissioner must maintain a publicly accessible child welfare data dashboard with monthly updates on system-wide issues.

Required Data Elements:

  • System-level performance indicators: Intake hotline, field investigation, case management, out-of-home placement
  • Federally mandated indicators: Time to first contact with all children, children in non-placement/temporary lodging status
  • Child fatality/near-fatality information: Within 48 hours, including date, sex, and age; link to Critical Incident Review Team reports within 24 hours
  • Workforce information: Child protective services staff hired/not yet trained, adoption worker vacancies, home finder vacancies

Data Accessibility Features:

  • Point-in-time numbers with trend analysis
  • Searchable by reporting date and county (starting July 1, 2026)
  • Data suppression permitted to protect individual identification

This requirement emphasizes transparency and accountability in the state's child welfare system.

Article 12: Parents' Bill of Rights

§49-12-1: Short Title

File Code: §49-12-1

This article is known and may be cited as the "Parents' Bill of Rights"

§49-12-2: Legislative Findings and Definition

File Code: §49-12-2

The Legislature finds that it is a fundamental right of parents to direct the upbringing, education, care, and medical care of their minor children.

Important information relating to a child should not be withheld—either inadvertently or purposefully—from the parent, including information relating to:

  • Health and well-being
  • Education
  • Other information while the child is in school district custody
"Parent" means a person who has legal custody of a minor child as a natural or adoptive parent or legal guardian.

§49-12-3: Infringement of Parental Rights

File Code: §49-12-3

The state, its subdivisions, governmental entities, or institutions may not infringe on fundamental parental rights without demonstrating:

  1. Reasonableness and necessity for a compelling state interest
  2. Narrow tailoring of the action
  3. No less restrictive means available to achieve the interest

§49-12-4: Enumerated Parental Rights

File Code: §49-12-4
All parental rights are reserved to the parent without obstruction or interference, including:
1. Right to Direct Education and Care

Parents have the right to direct the education and care of their minor child.

2. Right to Direct Upbringing and Moral/Religious Training

Parents have the right to direct upbringing and the moral or religious training of their minor child.

3. Right to Choose Educational Setting

Parents have the right to apply to enroll their minor child in:

  • Public school
  • Private/religious school
  • Home education program
  • Other available options authorized by law
4. Right to Access School Records

Parents have the right to access and review all school records relating to their minor child.

5. Right to Make Health Care Decisions

Parents have the right to make health care decisions for their minor child, unless otherwise prohibited by law.

Non-Discrimination Clause

The right to parental rights guaranteed by this article shall not be denied or abridged on account of disability.

Legal Remedies

A parent may raise this article as a defense before any court or administrative tribunal. Any person aggrieved may bring an action for injunctive relief in circuit court against a person engaging in conduct violating this article. The court may grant:

  • Temporary restraining orders
  • Preliminary injunctions
  • Permanent injunctions

Summary: Core Purposes and Intent

The West Virginia Child Welfare Act Seeks To:

  • Assure safety, care, and guidance for all children
  • Serve child welfare physically and mentally
  • Preserve family ties when possible
  • Respect fundamental rights of both children and parents
  • Provide family-centered rather than individual-centered services
  • Use community-based, least restrictive interventions
  • Prevent abuse and neglect through early identification
  • Maintain transparency and accountability to the public
  • Protect parental rights while ensuring child safety

The code balances child protection with parental rights, establishing a framework where:

  • Child welfare is paramount
  • Parents retain fundamental rights unless compelling state interest requires intervention
  • Interventions must be narrowly tailored and use least restrictive means
  • Services favor community-based and family-centered approaches
  • The state must operate with transparency and accountability

Important Legal Terminology

Key Legal Concepts

"Reasonable and Necessary": A legal standard requiring that state action infringing on parental rights must be both reasonable (practical/sensible) and necessary (essential, not optional).
"Compelling State Interest": A legal standard where the government has an overriding objective of utmost importance. Child safety is recognized as a compelling state interest in West Virginia law.
"Narrowly Tailored": A legal standard requiring that any government action be the least broad/restrictive means necessary to achieve the compelling interest.
"Least Restrictive Setting": A principle requiring that services be provided in environments that impose the fewest limitations on freedom possible while meeting the child's needs.
"Family-Centered": An approach to service provision that views the family as a unit and develops interventions addressing family-level issues rather than individual problems.
"Injunctive Relief": A court order (restraining order, preliminary or permanent injunction) requiring a person to stop or start specific conduct.
